Only important and kind of organized information 

Main idea: recombination of atomic hydrogen into molecular one creates heat. This happens when the hydrogen beam hits a platinum wire, so the heat goes into the wire. Heat increases the wires resistance and therefore we can see a voltage trop, from the current flowing through the wire. This signal goes back to the disassosiating RF oscillator 

The claim to be up to a tenth of a percent good. Meaning the signal of the changed hydrogen wire flux goes back to RF oscillator very fast => fast detection/feedback

# His setup 
He has: 
- diameter of wire $d = 2.5 \mu m$ (platinum!)
- length $l = 1.2cm$
- resistance at room temperature $R = 250 \Omega$ 
- background pressure < $10^{-4} Torr$ 
- time constant (time for signal to decay) $\tau = \frac{1}{2}s$ 

# Equations 
Temperature change from recombination of hydrogen $$\Delta T = \frac{2 n E (a^2 - x^2)}{\sigma \pi d}$$
Voltage change from recombination $$\Delta V = \frac{4 I R_0 \alpha n E a^2}{3 \sigma \pi d}$$	$x$ : distance from the centre of the wire 
	$\alpha$ : coefficient of resistance (depends on temperature)
	$n$ : number of atomic hydrogen per second, per crosssection are of beam 
	d : diameter of wire 
	$2a$ : length of wire 
	$\sigma$ : thermal conductivity 

# Notes and warnings 
- Teflon doesn't allow recombination 
- bridge oscillator (gives current) 
- RF oscillator can impact detection => good isolation important 
- wire can be less effective becaus it absorbs background gas 

# Results 
from $2 \cdot 10^{13} \frac{at}{cm^2 \cdot sec}$ of hydrogen beam they got $3 \mu V$  of voltage change
