there are different kinds of definiteness
# **positive semidefinite**
if the inner/scalar product of the vector with itself can be zero, even if the vector wasn't. $$\exists \hat v \neq 0: \langle \hat v, \hat v \rangle =0$$
# **indefinite**
if scalar product can be negative 
# **degeneracy** 
property of a vector . It is a vector that does not fulfil the normal inner/scalar product. If we wanted an indefinite inner product, but one vector does not fit this description, then this one vector is called degenerate

Generally I don't think  any of those kinds of scalar/inner products are relevant for physics and their application. Maybe except for some things in relativity and deep mathematics. 

# complex inner product 
$$\langle \space, \space \rangle : V \times V \to \mathbb{C}, \space (\hat v, \hat w) \mapsto \langle \hat v, \hat w \rangle$$
has the same properties as the normal scalar product, having the properties of symmetry, linearity, positive definiteness 
with the difference of the linearity having the complex component. $$\langle a \hat v, \hat w \rangle = \overline a \langle \hat v, \hat w \rangle$$ 
It is just a normal inner product, that is connected to a complex vector space, which is called then the **unitary vector space** $(V, \langle \space , \space \rangle)$
