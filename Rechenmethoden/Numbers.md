$$\mathbb{N} \subset \mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{Q}  \subset \mathbb{R}  \subset \mathbb{C} $$
# Integers 
$$\mathbb{Z}$$
$(\mathbb{Z}, +, \cdot)$is not a [[Field]], as the inverse of addition or multiplication is not defined inside the integers, but it is still a [[Group]]. 

# Rational numbers 
$$\mathbb{Q} = \{\frac{q}{p}| p \in \mathbb{Z}, p \neq 0\}$$
This defines the division using only integers, which now makes the rational numbers a field. It is a strict subset of the real numbers

# Irrational numbers
$$r \notin \mathbb{Q}$$
Those are all the fractions, that only have an approximations in the real numbers, but are precise only as a division. For example $\frac{1}{3} \approx 0.3333$
This number is technically infinite. The same way $\sqrt{2}$ being $= \frac{114142}{10000}$ is exact only as a fraction. 

# Real numbers
$$\mathbb{R}$$The step up from the rational numbers is the fact that real numbers are **continuous**, instead of the discrete number sequence of the rational ones. 

# Complex numbers 
$$\mathbb{C} \equiv \{z = x + iy| x,y \in \mathbb{R}\}$$
What makes this field of numbers special is the definition of the **imaginary unit** i: $$\sqrt{-1} =i \Rightarrow \space i^2 = -1$$This allows to define the complex number $z$ to have an imaginary and complex part
 

## complex conjugation
It is the "inverted" complex number $$\overline z \equiv x - iy$$ Which is useful to get rid of the imaginary part, for example if it is on the bottom of a fraction $$z \overline z = x^2 + y^2$$

## polar representation 
$$z = |z| \cdot (cos \phi + i sin \phi)$$
There we interpret the complex number as a vector starting at the origin. Being defined by the radius/length and the angle. It is also easy to convert back to a cartesian representation. 
The polar representation is similar in its build to the unit circle, that we use to determine sin and cos relations.
