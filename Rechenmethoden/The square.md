This square visualizes the way a matrix and a map act on the basis vectors and generally show the underlying structure of the whole process.  
On top the square, we have the map between the two vector spaces $V$ and $W$, denoted with the $\hat A$. A level down is the same operation, but on the level of the according basis vectors of those vector spaces. One upper right side we redefine the vector that comes from applying the map to the argument, as $\hat u_j \equiv A v_j = \hat w_i A^i_j$. The notation of having the vector on different sides is because of the nature of the matrix product with the vector. Then going down there are on both sides functions $\phi_{\hat v}$ and $\phi_{\hat w}$, that will take both vector spaces to the [[standard basis]]. While it is easy for $V$, as it goes directly to $\vec e_j$, it is harder for the other side, as it has the applied $\hat A$ first. Therefore the function $f_i$ is defined and preforms basically the same action. And in the end we have it represented as a matrix, that connects two sets of numbers. 
Not only is the definition and therefore the difference between a map and a matrix more visible in this square, it is also clear from the structure, that the **matrix is dependant on the chosen basis, while the map isn't**. 
**The matrix is just a representation of the linear map**
