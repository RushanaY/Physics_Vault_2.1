### Measurement of Raman scattering 
-> object gets radiated with laser light => scatterd light gets analyzed 

- because $\omega_{opt} (q) \approx const$, it doesn't matter from which direction we look at the spectrum 
- for the intencity of the scattering we have $$I \sim \omega_0^4 I_0 N (\frac{\partial \alpha}{\partial q})^2$$
	where we have $\omega_0$ be the frequency of exited laser, $I_0$ is the intensity of exited laer, $N$ the number of scatterd molecules/[[elementarzelle]] and the $(\frac{\partial \alpha}{\partial q})^2$ is the change in polarization 
- frequency of light is important for Raman intensity 
	- -> too hight of a frequency destroys the probe or leads to fluorescense 
	- -> the most used frequency os the IR 
Polarization: more polarization with higher period (Sn is more polarized than C)

### Determining the temperature 
-> proble gets radiated with laser light and the satterd light gets analysed 
- very thin spectras means that we have very sharp resolution 

Example: Temperature
- raman scattering intensity of a mode $\omega_m$ is proportional to the occupation of the beginning state and a $\omega^4$ factor (Dipolstrahlungsfeld) 
- Stokes $$I_S \propto (n +1) \omega_S^4$$
- Anti stokes $$I_{AS} \propto n \omega_{AS}^4$$
- => $$\frac{I_{AS}}{I_S} \approx exp(- \frac{\hbar \omega_m}{k_B T})$$
