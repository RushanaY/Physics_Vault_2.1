## 1. Noether's Theorem: Symmetry and Conservation

$$Symmetrie \leftrightarrow Erhaltungsgr\ddot{o} \beta e$$

This is the most profound connection in all of physics. Named after Emmy Noether, this principle states that every **symmetry** in nature corresponds to a **conservation law**.

- **Continuous Symmetries:**
    
    - **Time Symmetry:** If the laws of physics don't change if you do an experiment today vs. tomorrow, **Energy** is conserved.
        
    - **Translation Symmetry:** If physics is the same in Berlin as it is in Tokyo, **Momentum** is conserved.
        
    - **Rotational Symmetry:** If physics doesn't care which way you are facing, **Angular Momentum** is conserved.
        

**Exam Question Strategy:** If a question asks "Why is baryon number or charge conserved?", you should point to the underlying mathematical symmetry (often called "Gauge Symmetry") that requires that specific quantity to remain constant.

## 2. Discrete Symmetries: C, P, and T

These are "flip" symmetries that we use to test the fundamental forces.

- **C (Charge Conjugation):** Swapping every particle with its antiparticle (flipping charge).
    
- **P (Parity):** "Mirror symmetry." Flipping all spatial coordinates ($x, y, z \rightarrow -x, -y, -z$).
    
- **T (Time Reversal):** Running the movie backward ($t \rightarrow -t$).
    

**The Physics:** Most forces (Gravity, Electromagnetism, Strong Force) respect these symmetries. However, the **Weak Force** famously violates P-symmetry—it only "talks" to left-handed particles!

---

## 3. The CPT Theorem

$$Alle \text{ Wechselwirkungen sind invariant unter gemeinsamer Anwendung von } \hat{C}, \hat{P}, \text{ und } \hat{T}$$

This is the "ultimate guardrail" of physics. While nature might violate C, P, or T individually (or even CP together in some rare cases), it appears to **never** violate all three at once.

- **The Law:** If you flip the charge (**C**), mirror the image (**P**), and reverse the time (**T**) all at once, the resulting universe must behave exactly like ours.
    
- **Mathematical Necessity:** This isn't just a guess; it is a mathematical requirement for any consistent quantum field theory that respects Einstein's Special Relativity.
    

**Exam Question:** "What are the consequences of CPT invariance?"

**How to answer:** It guarantees that **particles and antiparticles have exactly the same mass, same lifetime, and opposite but equal magnetic moments**. If we ever found a difference between the mass of a proton and an antiproton, the CPT theorem (and much of modern physics) would crumble.
