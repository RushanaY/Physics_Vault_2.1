This question is about classifying particles called **Mesons**. Mesons are made of a quark and an antiquark pair ($q\bar{q}$). Specifically, we are looking at the "Ground State Pseudoscalar Mesons."

- **"Ground State"** means the quarks have no orbital angular momentum ($L=0$).
    
- **"Pseudoscalar"** means the spins are anti-aligned ($\uparrow\downarrow$), so total Spin $S=0$.

### (a) Identify the Mesons with Strangeness -1

**The Professor's Logic:**

We need to build a particle with Strangeness $S = -1$.

- The **Strange quark ($s$)** has $S = -1$.
    
- The **Anti-Strange quark ($\bar{s}$)** has $S = +1$.
    
- Light quarks ($u, d$) have $S=0$.
    

To get a total of $S = -1$, our meson _must_ contain exactly one **strange quark ($s$)**.

Since mesons are quark-antiquark pairs, the partner must be a **light antiquark** ($\bar{u}$ or $\bar{d}$). This gives us exactly two combinations.

**The Two Candidates:**

1. **Combination 1: $s\bar{u}$**
    
    - **Charge:** $s (-1/3) + \bar{u} (-2/3) = -1$.
        
    - **Name:** This is the **Negative Kaon ($K^-$)**.
        
2. **Combination 2: $s\bar{d}$**
    
    - **Charge:** $s (-1/3) + \bar{d} (+1/3) = 0$.
        
    - **Name:** This is the **Anti-Neutral Kaon ($\bar{K}^0$)**.
        
        _(Note: The regular $K^0$ is $d\bar{s}$, which has $S=+1$. This is a very common student mistake!)_
        

**Isospin ($I$) and its 3rd Component ($I_3$):**

In particle physics, particles that are identical except for their electric charge are grouped into "multiplets." The Kaons form a doublet (a pair).

- **Total Isospin ($I$):** Since there are 2 states in this group, $2I + 1 = 2$, which means **$I = 1/2$**.
    
- **3rd Component ($I_3$):** By convention, the member with the _higher_ electric charge gets the higher $I_3$.
    
    - $\bar{K}^0$ (Charge 0) is higher than $K^-$ (Charge -1).
        
    - So $\bar{K}^0$ gets $+1/2$ and $K^-$ gets $-1/2$.
        

**The Answer Table:**

|**Particle Designation**|**Quark Composition**|**Strong Isospin (I)**|**Component (I3​)**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**$K^-$ (Negative Kaon)**|$s\bar{u}$|$1/2$|$-1/2$|
|**$\bar{K}^0$ (Anti-Kaon Zero)**|$s\bar{d}$|$1/2$|$+1/2$|

### (b) Via which interaction do these mesons decay? Justify your answer.

**The Professor's Insight:**

This is a question about conservation laws.

We have a Kaon ($S = -1$). We want it to decay into something else.

But the Kaons are the **lightest** particles that contain a strange quark.

- **Can they decay via the Strong Force?**
    
    The Strong force _must_ conserve Strangeness. To decay strongly, a Kaon would have to break apart into lighter strange particles. But there _are_ no lighter strange particles! (The Kaon mass is $\approx 493$ MeV, while the Lambda baryon is $\approx 1115$ MeV).
    
    _Verdict: No._
    
- **Can they decay via the Electromagnetic Force?**
    
    The EM force also conserves Strangeness. It can create photons ($\gamma$), but it cannot turn a strange quark into a down quark.
    
    _Verdict: No._
    
- **The Only Option Left: The Weak Force.**
    
    The Weak Interaction is the only force in the universe capable of **changing quark flavor**. It can turn a Strange quark ($s$) into an Up quark ($u$). This changes the Strangeness ($S$) by 1 unit ($\Delta S = 1$), which is forbidden in Strong/EM but allowed in Weak interactions.
    

**The Answer:**

These mesons decay via the **Weak Interaction**.

**Justification:**

1. **Conservation of Strangeness:** The Strong and Electromagnetic interactions conserve Strangeness.
    
2. **Lightest Strange Particle:** The Kaons ($K^-, \bar{K}^0$) are the lightest particles containing a strange quark. Therefore, they cannot decay into other strange particles (which would conserve Strangeness).
    
3. **Flavor Change:** To decay, the strange quark ($s$) must convert into a lighter non-strange quark ($u$ or $d$). This flavor-changing process violates Strangeness conservation and is only possible via the Weak Interaction.
