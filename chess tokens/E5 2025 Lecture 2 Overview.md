### 1. The Building Blocks: Nucleons

First, we must understand the constituents. The nucleus is made of **nucleons**, which are protons and neutrons.

- **Protons ($p$):** Have a positive charge ($+e$) and a mass of roughly $938.27 \text{ MeV}/c^2$.
    
- **Neutrons ($n$):** Are neutral (0 charge) and slightly heavier than protons, with a mass of roughly $939.56 \text{ MeV}/c^2$.
    
- **Spin:** Both are fermions with spin $1/2$.
    

**Exam Tip:** Note that the neutron is slightly heavier than the proton. This mass difference is crucial because free neutrons are unstable and decay into protons (beta decay), with a lifetime of about 880 seconds.

**Magnetic Moments**

You might expect the neutron, being neutral, to have zero magnetic moment. However, it has a significant negative magnetic moment ($-1.91 \mu_N$). This was one of the first clues that nucleons are not fundamental particles but have an internal structure (quarks).

### 2. Nuclear Nomenclature

To speak the language of nuclear physics, you need to know these definitions:

- **$Z$ (Atomic Number):** Number of protons. Defines the element.
    
- **$N$ (Neutron Number):** Number of neutrons.
    
- **$A$ (Mass Number):** Total number of nucleons, $A = Z + N$.
    

**Classifications you must memorize:**

- **Isotopes:** Same $Z$, different $N$ (e.g., Carbon-12 and Carbon-14).
    
- **Isobars:** Same $A$, different $Z$.
    
- **Isotones:** Same $N$, different $Z$.
    
- **Mirror Nuclei:** Pairs where $Z$ and $N$ are swapped (e.g., ${}^{3}_{1}\text{H}$ and ${}^{3}_{2}\text{He}$). These are important because they show us that the strong nuclear force is nearly the same for protons and neutrons (charge independence).
    

### 3. Binding Energy and Mass Defect

This is the "energy currency" of the nucleus. The mass of a nucleus is **less** than the sum of its parts. This missing mass is converted into binding energy ($B$) that holds the nucleus together.

- **The Formula:**
    
    $$M_{nucleus} = Z \cdot m_p + N \cdot m_n - B/c^2$$
    
- **Binding Energy per Nucleon ($B/A$):** On average, this is about $8 \text{ MeV}$ per nucleon.
    

**The Binding Energy Curve:**

This is one of the most famous graphs in physics.

- It rises sharply for light nuclei.
    
- It peaks around Iron-56 ($^{56}\text{Fe}$), which is the most stable nucleus.
    
- It slowly decreases for heavy nuclei. This explains why **fusion** works for light elements (moving up the curve toward iron) and **fission** works for heavy elements (splitting down toward iron).
    

### 4. Models of the Nucleus (High Exam Priority)

Physicists use different models to explain different behaviors. You need to know three major ones from these notes.

#### A. The Liquid Drop Model (Tröpfchenmodell)

This model treats the nucleus like a drop of incompressible fluid. It leads to the **Weizsäcker Mass Formula** (Semi-Empirical Mass Formula), which calculates binding energy ($B$). You must know the five terms:

1. **Volume Term ($+a_v A$):** The dominant attractive force. Binding energy is proportional to the volume (or $A$) because the strong force is short-range.
    
2. **Surface Term ($-a_o A^{2/3}$):** Nucleons on the surface have fewer neighbors to hold onto, reducing stability. This is proportional to surface area ($\sim r^2 \sim A^{2/3}$).
    
3. **Coulomb Term ($-a_c \frac{Z(Z-1)}{A^{1/3}}$):** Protons repel each other. This reduces stability, especially in large nuclei.
    
4. **Asymmetry Term ($-a_a \frac{(N-Z)^2}{A}$):** The nucleus "prefers" to have equal numbers of protons and neutrons ($N=Z$). Imbalance costs energy.
    
5. **Pairing Term ($+\delta$):** Nuclei with even numbers of protons and neutrons (even-even) are more stable than odd-odd nuclei due to spin pairing.
    

#### B. The Fermi Gas Model

This model treats protons and neutrons as independent gases of fermions moving in a potential well.

- It explains the **Asymmetry Term**. If you have too many neutrons, you have to stack them into higher and higher energy levels (Fermi levels), which costs energy.
    
- It assumes a potential well depth ($V_0$) of roughly $40 \text{ MeV}$ and a Fermi energy ($E_F$) of $33 \text{ MeV}$.
    

#### C. The Shell Model (Schalenmodell)

The Liquid Drop model fails to explain "Magic Numbers." Experimentally, nuclei with specific numbers of protons or neutrons (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) are exceptionally stable.

To explain this, we use the Shell Model, similar to electron shells in atoms.

1. **Potentials:** We start with a Harmonic Oscillator or "Woods-Saxon" potential. However, these alone **do not** predict the correct magic numbers (like 28 or 50).
    
2. **Spin-Orbit Coupling (The Fix):** We must add a strong interaction between the nucleon's spin and its orbital angular momentum (Spin-Bahn-Kopplung).
    
    - This splits the energy levels significantly.
        
    - This splitting creates large energy gaps exactly at the magic numbers: 28, 50, 82, 126.
        

**Exam Critical:** If asked "How do we derive the magic numbers?", the answer is **The Shell Model with Strong Spin-Orbit Coupling.**

### 5. The Valley of Stability

You see the "Z-N Diagram" or "Chart of Nuclides" in your notes.

- For light nuclei, stable atoms lie on the line $N=Z$.
    
- For heavier nuclei, the stable path (Valley of Stability) bends upward, meaning $N > Z$.
    
- **Why?** As the number of protons ($Z$) increases, the Coulomb repulsion grows rapidly. To keep the nucleus from flying apart, nature adds extra neutrons to provide more attractive "strong force" glue without adding more repulsive electric charge.
    

We can derive the most stable $Z$ for a given $A$ by minimizing the mass in the Weizsäcker formula:

$$Z \approx \frac{A}{2} \frac{1}{1 + \text{correction term}}$$
