Think of the nucleus not as a static ball, but as a complex "drop" of quantum fluid. Here is a breakdown of your new formulas:

---

## 1. Nuclear Mass (Kernmasse)

This formula calculates the total mass of a specific nucleus.

$$M_k = Z \cdot m_p + N \cdot m_n - B/c^2$$

- **The Letters:**
    
    - $M_k$: The total mass of the nucleus.
        
    - $Z$: The number of protons (atomic number).
        
    - $m_p$: The mass of a single proton.
        
    - $N$: The number of neutrons.
        
    - $m_n$: The mass of a single neutron.
        
    - $B$: The **Binding Energy**.
        
    - $c$: The speed of light.
        
- **What it's for:** You might notice something strange: if you add up the individual protons and neutrons, the sum is actually _heavier_ than the final nucleus! That "missing" mass ($B/c^2$) is the energy that was released to bind the nucleons together. This is a direct application of Einstein's $E=mc^2$.
    

---

## 2. Nuclear Radius

How big is a nucleus? Since nucleons (protons and neutrons) are packed tightly like drops in a liquid, the volume of the nucleus is proportional to the number of particles inside.

$$r = r_0 \sqrt[3]{A}, \quad r_0 = 1,2 \text{ fm}$$

- **The Letters:**
    
    - $r$: The radius of the nucleus.
        
    - $A$: The mass number (total number of protons + neutrons, $Z+N$).
        
    - $r_0$: A constant (approximately 1.2 femtometers) representing the effective radius of a single nucleon.
        
- **What it's for:** This allows us to estimate the size of any atom's nucleus just by knowing how many nucleons it has. It tells us that the density of nuclear matter is roughly constant across different elements.
    

---

## 3. The Weizsäcker Mass Formula

This is a famous formula based on the **Liquid Drop Model** (Tröpfchenmodell). It predicts the binding energy ($B$) by treating the nucleus like a droplet of liquid with different forces acting on it.

$$B = +a_v A - a_o A^{2/3} - a_c \frac{Z(Z-1)}{A^{1/3}} - a_a \frac{(N-Z)^2}{A} - a_p \frac{\delta}{A^{1/2}}$$

- **The Terms (What they represent):**
    
    1. **Volumenenergie ($+a_v A$):** The "glue." Each nucleon is attracted to its neighbors by the strong nuclear force.
        
    2. **Oberflächenenergie ($-a_o A^{2/3}$):** A correction. Nucleons on the surface have fewer neighbors to cling to, which reduces the total binding.
        
    3. **Coulombenergie ($-a_c \dots$):** Protons are all positively charged and push each other away. This electrical repulsion weakens the nucleus.
        
    4. **Asymmetrieenergie ($-a_a \dots$):** Nature prefers balance. If there are far more neutrons than protons (or vice-versa), the nucleus becomes less stable.
        
    5. **Paarungsenergie ($-a_p \dots$):** Nucleons like to be in pairs. Even numbers of protons and neutrons are more stable than odd numbers.
        

---

## 4. Woods-Saxon Potential

While the liquid drop model is great for mass, the **Woods-Saxon Potential** describes the "well" or "trap" that holds a single nucleon inside the nucleus.

$$V_{ws}(r) = \frac{-V_0}{1 + e^{(r-R)/a}}$$

- **The Letters:**
    
    - $V_{ws}(r)$: The potential energy at a distance $r$ from the center.
        
    - $V_0$: The depth of the potential well.
        
    - $R$: The nuclear radius.
        
    - $a$: The "skin thickness" or diffuseness, representing how blurry the edge of the nucleus is (approx. 0.7 fm).
        
- **What it's for:** This is a more realistic version of a simple "box" potential. It is used in the **Shell Model** to calculate the specific energy levels of nucleons, helping us understand "magic numbers" where nuclei are extra stable.
    

It’s quite a bit to take in, but these formulas essentially tell the story of a tug-of-war between the **Strong Force** trying to hold the "drop" together and the **Coulomb Force** trying to fly it apart!
