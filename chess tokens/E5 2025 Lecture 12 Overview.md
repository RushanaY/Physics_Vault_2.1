The **Electroweak Interaction** is one of the most beautiful but mathematically dense chapters in particle physics. It represents a major triumph of the 20th century: realizing that two seemingly different forces—Electromagnetism and the Weak Nuclear Force—are actually two sides of the same coin.

Let's break down your lecture notes into the key concepts you need to master for your exam.

### 1. From Fermi Theory to W Bosons

In the early days, we described weak interactions (like beta decay) using **Fermi Theory**, which assumed a point-like interaction. However, your notes highlight a problem: this theory breaks down at high energies (specifically, it violates the unitarity limit).

To fix this, we introduce **Intermediate Vector Bosons**. Instead of a point interaction, particles exchange massive bosons: the $W^+$ and $W^-$.

- **Mass Matters:** Unlike the massless photon in electromagnetism, W bosons are very heavy ($\mu_W \approx 80.6 \text{ GeV}/c^2$).
    
- **Short Range:** Because the W boson is so massive, the "propagator" term in the equation suppresses the interaction at low energies. This explains why the weak force is so... weak. It acts over a very short range.
    

### 2. Weak Isospin and Parity Violation

This is a crucial concept for your exam. The weak force is unique because it **does not treat left-handed and right-handed particles equally**.

- **The Doublets:** The W boson only couples to **left-handed** particles (like $e_L$) and right-handed antiparticles. We group these left-handed particles into "doublets" with a quantum number called **Weak Isospin ($T$)**.
    
    - For example, the electron ($e_L$) and electron-neutrino ($\nu_{e,L}$) form a doublet.
        
    - The neutrino has $T_3 = +1/2$ and the electron has $T_3 = -1/2$.
        
- **The Singlets:** Right-handed particles (like $e_R$) do not feel the charged weak force. They are "singlets" with $T=0$.
    
- **Quark Mixing:** The weak force also talks to quarks, but with a twist. It interacts with "mixed" states ($d', s', b'$) rather than the physical mass states ($d, s, b$).
    

### 3. The Neutral Current ($Z^0$ Boson)

For a long time, we only knew about "Charged Currents" (CC)—interactions involving $W^\pm$ that change the charge of a particle (like turning a neutron into a proton).

Your notes introduce a third boson: the **$Z^0$**. This mediates **Neutral Currents (NC)**, where particles interact via the weak force but _without_ changing their electric charge.

- **Discovery:** The existence of these neutral currents was a huge prediction. They were first observed in the **Gargamelle Experiment** at CERN in 1973. This experiment used a massive bubble chamber filled with Freon to detect neutrino scattering.
    

### 4. The GSW Theory (Electroweak Unification)

This is the heart of the "Standard Model." The **Glashow-Salam-Weinberg (GSW) Theory** unifies the electromagnetic and weak forces.

How does it work?

1. We start with two mathematical fields: a triplet $W$ (associated with weak isospin) and a singlet $B^0$ (associated with weak hypercharge).
    
2. **The Mixing:** The physical photon ($\gamma$) and the $Z^0$ boson are actually mixtures of these $W^0$ and $B^0$ fields.
    
    - The photon ($\gamma$) is massless ($\mu_\gamma = 0$).
        
    - The $Z^0$ is massive ($\mu_Z \approx 91.2 \text{ GeV}$).
        
3. **The Weinberg Angle ($\theta_W$):** The specific mix is determined by the "weak mixing angle" or Weinberg Angle.
    
    $$|\gamma\rangle = \sin\theta_W |W^0\rangle + \cos\theta_W |B^0\rangle$$
    
    $$|Z^0\rangle = \cos\theta_W |W^0\rangle - \sin\theta_W |B^0\rangle$$
    

**Key Exam Relationship:**

There is a strict mathematical relation between the electric charge $e$, the weak coupling $g$, and the hypercharge coupling $g'$:

$$e = g \sin\theta_W = g' \cos\theta_W$$

Experimentally, $\sin^2\theta_W \approx 0.23$.

### 5. Experimental Verification (The Z Peak)

The notes highlight the **LEP (Large Electron-Positron) collider** at CERN. This machine collided electrons and positrons to create millions of $Z^0$ bosons.

- **The Resonance Curve:** By scanning the energy, physicists mapped out the "resonance curve" of the $Z^0$ boson.
    
- **Counting Neutrinos:** The _width_ of this curve (how broad the peak is) tells us how quickly the $Z^0$ decays. Since the $Z^0$ can decay into neutrinos ($\nu \bar{\nu}$), the width depends on how many neutrino types exist.
    
- **The Result:** The data fits perfectly with **3 generations of light neutrinos**. If there were a 4th neutrino, the peak would be wider and lower.
    

### 6. The Higgs Mechanism

Finally, your notes address a major theoretical problem: If we just write down the equations for symmetry, the $W$ and $Z$ bosons _should_ be massless (like the photon). But we know they are heavy.

The solution is **Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking** (the Higgs Mechanism).

- **High Energy:** At very high energies, the symmetry is perfect, and all bosons are massless.
    
- **Low Energy:** At lower energies (our universe today), the symmetry "breaks." The Higgs field gives mass to the $W^\pm$ and $Z^0$ bosons, while leaving the photon massless.

### Professor's Exam Cheat Sheet

If you see these on the exam, here is what you need to know:

- **$W^\pm$**: Charged carrier, changes particle flavor, couples only to left-handed particles ($e_L$).
    
- **$Z^0$**: Neutral carrier, couples to both left and right (but unequally), mix of $W^0$ and $B^0$.
    
- **Weinberg Angle ($\theta_W$)**: The parameter that defines how the weak and electromagnetic forces mix. $\tan\theta_W = g'/g$.
    
- **$N_\nu = 3$**: The LEP experiments proved there are exactly 3 light neutrino families.
